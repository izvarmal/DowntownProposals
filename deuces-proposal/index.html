The situation

A corridor with history,
waiting on a project
that stalled.

The Deuces — 22nd Street South — is one of St. Petersburg's most historically significant commercial corridors. It has been planned around, promised to, and invested in by others. The businesses on the ground have been waiting for the outside world to activate them. This program changes that direction entirely.

Past investment focus
Stadium-related streetscape — now stalled

Capital improvement investment in the corridor was tied to a stadium-related streetscape project that has since stalled. Infrastructure-first thinking left the businesses — the actual economic engine of 22nd Street — without design support, visual standards, or program resources.

Core pain point
Unfinished momentum on a historic street

The stalled project left a visible gap between what the corridor was promised and what it looks like today. Businesses are present but visually undefined — lacking the signage, facade quality, and street presence that signals to the market that 22nd Street is open, active, and worth visiting.

The strategic opening
Activate from within, not from above

Ground-floor activation doesn't require the streetscape project to resume. It creates momentum that makes future infrastructure investment more defensible — and more visible — when it eventually arrives. This program doesn't wait on a timeline. It sets one.

The equity dimension
Removing barriers for historically underinvested businesses

For a corridor with historically underinvested businesses, the 100% in-kind model is not just efficient — it is equitable. Business owners without capital, contractor relationships, or procurement experience participate on equal footing. The grant removes every barrier that has previously kept this kind of improvement out of reach.

The streetscape project gave 22nd Street a place on a map. This program gives it an identity on the street — one built by the businesses and community that have always been here, not by an infrastructure timeline that keeps moving.

The design principle for this corridor

This is not generic revitalization. The Deuces has a cultural identity that predates and outlasts any infrastructure project. The visual standards developed in Phase 1 are built from the specific history, architecture, and community character of 22nd Street — not borrowed from another downtown. What this corridor looks like should be decided by the people who built it.

How the program works
CRA role
Lump-sum grant to Main Street

The CRA provides a single grant to the Main Street organization — covering design, permitting, contractor hiring, and implementation. One check. One recipient. Clean accountability to the board.

Main Street role
Program manager & implementer

Main Street manages the process from application through construction close-out — contractor hiring, permits, design coordination, and CRA reporting. This program transfers fully to Main Street's operational ownership.

Business owner role
Approve designs. Receive improvements.

Business owners apply, participate in their design session, and approve the final plan. They do not manage money, hire contractors, or navigate permits. The grant is 100% in-kind — no cash changes hands with the business.

Storefront Mastery role
Strategy, design & business consultation

We establish the corridor strategy, develop the visual identity framework, lead the business reimagining sessions, and remain available as a design resource during implementation. We are not the program manager — Main Street is.

Scope of work

Two phases.
Built for
22nd Street.

The program architecture is designed around the specific conditions of the Deuces — not a generic template. Phase 1 produces the corridor identity framework and the board-ready program operations document. Phase 2 activates individual businesses within it.

01
Strategic Corridor Framework
$8,500

Full corridor audit of 22nd Street South — boundary maps, Google Earth street-level analysis, business inventory, and visual baseline assessment. This phase focuses on identifying the specific blocks where concentrated storefront investment will produce the clearest visual signal, particularly in zones that the stalled streetscape project was meant to address. It maps current conditions honestly, ranks businesses by readiness and street visibility, and establishes a visual language for the corridor that reflects its historic African American identity — not a generic downtown aesthetic.

This phase also delivers the program operations framework — the board-ready document that explains how the 100% in-kind model works administratively. It covers how the grant flows from CRA to Main Street, how businesses are selected, how contractors are vetted, what compliance and reporting looks like, and how this program connects to — rather than competes with — the CRA's existing capital improvement agenda on the corridor.

Flat fee. Includes travel. Delivered remotely with one on-site visit for corridor walkthrough and ED alignment session.

02
Business Reimagining
$2,100 / business

Structured one-on-one engagement with each participating business owner. On the Deuces, this phase carries particular weight: many business owners have watched this corridor be talked about and planned around without being included in decisions about their own commercial environment. The reimagining session is not just a design conversation — it is the moment a business owner sees their storefront as a tool for their community's visibility and their own commercial growth.

Because Phase 1 has established the corridor identity and visual standards, each session is focused and efficient. Each business receives a before/after rendering aligned to the 22nd Street visual language and an implementation brief that Main Street uses to manage contractors, permits, and construction. The business owner approves. Everything else is handled.

Price per business is fixed regardless of program size. Businesses participate on equal footing regardless of their capital position — the in-kind model ensures access is not determined by resources.

Implementation phase
Managed by Main Street. Supported by Storefront Mastery.

Contractor procurement, permitting, construction management, and CRA compliance reporting are Main Street's operational responsibility — as grant recipient and program manager, they own implementation. Storefront Mastery remains available as a design and technical resource throughout: answering contractor questions, reviewing material selections, and ensuring implementation matches the approved designs. This is a consulting role, not a project management engagement, and it is not separately billed.
