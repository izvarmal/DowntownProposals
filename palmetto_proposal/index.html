The situation

Palmetto has invested
in everything except
the storefronts.

The CRA has done the right work — parks, historic buildings, public spaces. The civic infrastructure is in place. But foot traffic follows storefronts, not parks. There is an activation gap between Palmetto's public investment and its commercial corridor, and that gap is the opportunity this program addresses.

Past investment focus
Public space & historic preservation

The CRA has directed capital toward parks, civic infrastructure, and historic building restoration. These create the right backdrop — but the commercial layer in front of them has no visual standard, no program support, and no momentum.

Core pain point
Invisible businesses on a prepared street

Storefronts without signage, visual identity, and street presence fail to capture the foot traffic that public investment was designed to generate. The district looks improved from above but doesn't activate at eye level — which is the only level that matters to a customer.

What hasn't been tried
A business-facing, in-kind storefront program

No storefront grant program has existed in Palmetto. That is not a failure — it is an opportunity. The CRA can introduce a tool that directly connects public investment to business performance and creates a visible return on every dollar already spent.

The strategic opening
Complete the investment cycle

The corridor doesn't need more public infrastructure. It needs the commercial layer to meet what's already been built. A coordinated storefront program is not a new initiative — it is the final phase of the work already underway.

You've built the stage. The performers aren't ready for it yet. This program changes that — block by block, business by business, until the corridor earns the attention its public investment deserves.

How the program works
CRA role
Lump-sum grant to Main Street

The CRA provides a single grant to the Main Street organization — covering design, permitting, contractor hiring, and implementation. One check. One recipient. Clean accountability.

Main Street role
Program manager & implementer

Main Street manages the entire process from application through construction close-out — contractor hiring, permits, design coordination, and CRA reporting. This is their program to run.

Business owner role
Approve designs. Receive improvements.

Business owners apply, participate in their design session, and approve the final plan. They do not manage money, hire contractors, or navigate permits. The grant is 100% in-kind.

Storefront Mastery role
Strategy, design & business consultation

We establish the corridor strategy, design the visual standards, lead the business reimagining sessions, and remain available as a design resource during implementation. We are not the program manager — Main Street is.

Scope of work

Two phases.
One complete
program.

Phase 1 builds the strategic foundation and delivers the board-ready program framework. Phase 2 activates individual businesses within it. Together they take Palmetto from no storefront program to a fully operational, repeatable system.

01
Strategic Corridor Framework
$8,500

Full corridor audit of the Palmetto Main Street district — boundary maps, Google Earth street-level analysis, business inventory, and visual baseline assessment. Because this is Palmetto's first storefront program, Phase 1 does the foundational work that makes every downstream decision faster, cheaper, and more defensible: which block to pilot, which businesses are ready, what the corridor looks like today, and what a realistic grant-per-business number is for this specific street.

This phase also delivers the program operations framework — the document your board needs to understand and authorize the 100% in-kind model. It covers how the grant flows from CRA to Main Street, how businesses are selected, how contractors are vetted, and what compliance and reporting looks like. Designed to be taken directly into a board meeting.

Flat fee. Includes travel. Delivered remotely with one on-site visit for corridor walkthrough and ED alignment session.

02
Business Reimagining
$2,100 / business

Structured one-on-one engagement with each participating business owner. Because Phase 1 has already mapped the corridor and established visual standards, these sessions are focused and efficient — not exploratory. Each business owner gets a walkthrough interview, a before/after rendering of their storefront, and a scoped implementation brief specifying materials, signage, paint palette, and contractor responsibilities.

The business owner approves. Main Street executes. Individual improvements are already oriented toward the same corridor vision from Phase 1 — so they compound into district-wide visual coherence rather than isolated upgrades. Five improved storefronts on the same block is a transformed block. Ten is a transformed corridor.

Price per business is fixed regardless of program size. If 5 businesses participate: $10,500. If 10 participate: $21,000. The CRA can budget for a range with no surprises.

Implementation phase
Managed by Main Street. Supported by Storefront Mastery.

Contractor procurement, permitting, construction management, and CRA compliance reporting are Main Street's operational responsibility — as the grant recipient and program manager, they own implementation. Storefront Mastery remains available as a design and technical resource throughout: answering contractor questions, reviewing material selections, and ensuring implementation matches the approved designs. This is a consulting role, not a project management engagement, and it is not separately billed.
